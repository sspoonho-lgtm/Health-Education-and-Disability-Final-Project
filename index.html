Health Education and Disability

This project looks into the distribution of disability prevalence across the United States, along with an analysis of school health policy priorities. Our two data sets, linked below, show variations and concentrations of disability and health policy data across the United States based on geography and social factors. Our data and insights can be used by Federal or State Health Departments, or Non-Profit Disability Advocacy Groups to prioritize specific services to regions with the greatest need, such as those with high Mobility or Cognitive disability prevalence.

Visualization Three- Disability and Health Data

• Looking at the distributions, surveys for disability show the most frequent percentage of White respondents that had a disability was 3%-3.9%. A high frequency of a low percentage for disability means across states, people of White ethnicity usually had a minority amount of people who had a disability.

• People of Asian ethnicity had the least amount of distribution overall, with the most frequent disability percentage of Asian ethnicity as %4-%4.9. The low distribution compared to other ethnicities could indicate either a small population of Asian survey participants, or that surveys have possibly under-reported people of Asian background.

• Hispanic and Black ethnicities have a somewhat similar distribution for percentages of disabilities, where the most frequent reported percentage is 8%-8.9% for the Hispanic ethnicity and 7%-7.9% for the Black ethnicity - a one percent difference. The similar distributions could indicate possible similarities between Black and Hispanic ethnicities regarding history, prevalence, and background for disabilities.

Visualization Four- School Health Profiles Profiles

• The largest category of policies is for Health Education, which is 36% of the total, followed by professional development and preperation. This suggests that the majority of U.S. school health policy focus is placed on activities or programs that promote student health and a healthy school enviornment, alongside preparing students for their future jobs and the work force.

• Surprisingly, the category of Before and After School Programs is low, at only 0.89 percent. This may be due to the fact that instructional time for tested core subjects is a higher priority for teachers in schools, rather than providing a lot of assistance to activities outside of school hours. This puts mroe responsibility on working parents or families, where they are at work before/after school and cannot come to pick up or drop off their children at the normal time.

• States with a low policy distribution across key areas can use this national data to identify where they need to focus more on. By observing the areas where they fall behind, educational departments can prioritize putting more time into those smaller percentage areas.

Visualization Six- School Health Profiles Profiles

• Firstly, the map reveals distinct regional concentrations for mobility disability (dark purple), and cognative disability (light red). This may be due to the fact that aging populations or chronic conditions may be higher in these areas, as they lead to disability as the person ages.

• Another insight is there are only two states whose most prevelant disability is an independant living disability. This suggests that the barriers to independent living, which include difficulty doing errands, shopping, or visiting the doctor alone, are failing with community support in these states.

• From reigonal patterns seen in this data, we can use this to provide more support for each disability area. If disabilities are loosely grouped around by state, it is easier for reigons of the US to work together to provide more targeted care for these people.

Conclusion

One key takeaway from this project is that the disability analysis highlighted strong regional concentrations of Mobility and Cognitive disabilities, particularly in certain HHS regions and rural areas, which calls fot a specific and localized approach to support services. Additionally, our review of school health policies found a focus on Health Education, reflected in both the pie chart and choropleth, suggesting an effort to keep students healthy and make them aware of bodiy health. However, this focus is contrasted by the considerable amount of missing data across several states, representing major gaps in policy effectiveness and data gathering. The primary audiences who can use this insight include Federal Agencies, State and Local Education Departmentsor Non-Profit Disability Advocacy Groups, which can which can use the data for grants and funding, or better target their services to communities with specific disability concentrations.
